## Goal

Make gear geometry physically meaningful: gears only transmit motion when they
actually touch with compatible modules, rotate in consistent directions, and
their teeth visually interlock.

## Background

Today the solver propagates speed purely through stored `GearConnection`
records; positions are cosmetic. A gear dragged away from its train keeps
spinning. `GearNode.module` exists but is not enforced.

## Deliverables

- Pure `validateConnections(system)` in the simulation core: a mesh connection
  is valid only when both gears share a module and their center distance is
  within tolerance of the sum of pitch radii (`module * teeth / 2`); a compound
  connection is valid only when centers are concentric.
- Solver stops propagating through invalid connections; downstream gears stop.
- Canvas renders invalid connections distinctly (e.g. dashed/red) so breakage
  is visible while dragging.
- Snap-to-mesh on drag: dropping a gear near a candidate partner snaps its
  center so pitch circles are tangent.
- Direction-conflict (jam) detection: odd cycles in the mesh graph mark the
  system as jammed, surfaced in the UI as a teaching moment.
- Tooth-phase alignment: follower angle derived from driver angle, tooth
  ratio, and a phase constant fixed at mesh time so teeth interlock visually.

## Acceptance Criteria

- Dragging a meshed gear out of tangency stops its downstream train and shows
  the broken link; dragging it back within snap range restores motion.
- Gears with mismatched modules refuse to mesh.
- A 3-gear odd cycle reports a jam instead of solving contradictory
  directions.
- Meshed teeth visibly interleave during playback.
- All geometry checks live in the framework-agnostic simulation core with
  focused Vitest coverage; tolerances are named constants.

## Notes

- Research in progress; scope may split (validation/snap first, jam detection
  and tooth-phase polish second).
- Keep everything deterministic — no physics engine.
